Favourite-longshot Bias (The Surface)
Most traders never question the price.
If a market shows 8¢ on a longshot, they assume there's an 8% chance. If it shows 92¢ on a favourite, they assume 92%. The price is the probability.
It isn't.

Anchoring Bias (Going Deeper)
Most traders can't let go of a price.
A market trades at 30¢ for three weeks. The number burns into the trader's brain. Then the underlying reality shifts - new evidence, new data, a fundamental change that should push the market to 60¢. But it doesn't. The price drifts up to 35¢, maybe 40¢, and stalls.
Why?
Because thousands of traders are still anchored to 30¢. That's "the right price" in their head. Anything higher feels expensive. So they sell into every rally, capping the price at a level that no longer matches reality.
This is anchoring bias - and it's the slowest, most profitable inefficiency on Polymarket.
Why it works on Polymarket:
Prediction markets have a structural anchoring problem that stocks don't. A stock has earnings, cash flow, comps - multiple ways to re-anchor a "fair price" when reality changes. A Polymarket contract has only one anchor: the last price most people remember.
When that anchor is wrong, it stays wrong for days - sometimes weeks - before the market re-prices.
The pattern looks like this:
1. Market settles into a stable price
2. Real-world conditions shift
3. Crowd anchored to old price → fades every move
4. Price drifts slowly toward true value
5. Eventually re-anchors at new level
Step 3 is the trade. The longer the original anchor held, the longer the re-pricing takes - and the more time quants have to load the position.
The mental shortcut:The brain hates updating beliefs. Once a number gets locked in as "what this thing is worth," every new piece of information gets filtered through that anchor instead of evaluated on its own. Traders see the new evidence, but they discount it. They wait for "confirmation." They tell themselves the rally will fade.
By the time the anchor finally breaks, quants are already sitting on the position.

End-of-Event Bias (Deeper Still)
Some traders think a market is most efficient near resolution.
The opposite is true.
As a contract approaches expiry, three structural forces converge to break pricing - and they break it in ways that are mathematically predictable. Quants don't guess these dislocations. They model them.
This is end-of-event bias: the systematic mispricing that emerges in the final hours of a market's life. It's the most academically documented anomaly in prediction markets, with peer-reviewed studies on horse racing, sports books, and Iowa Electronic Markets all converging on the same finding - inefficiency increases as time-to-resolution decreases.
And almost no retail trader on Polymarket knows it exists.

End-of-event mispricing emerges from the interaction of three quantifiable forces:
1. Liquidity provider risk-off (LP withdrawal accelerates as T → 0)
2. Time-decayed information arrival (fewer new data points, more noise)
3. Forced-exit pressure (position holders unwind to avoid resolution risk)
These aren't behavioural quirks. They're structural features of how AMM-style prediction markets behave when the option-like character of the contract decays toward zero.

Reflexivity (The Edge of the Map)
Most traders think price reflects probability.
Actually, not always.
At the deepest level of the market, something stranger happens - price itself starts to influence probability. The act of trading changes the thing being traded. The map redraws the territory.
This is reflexivity - a concept George Soros built a $40 billion fortune on, and one almost no Polymarket trader has internalised.
In a perfectly efficient market, price is a passive observer. It reflects underlying reality without distorting it. In a reflexive market, price is an active participant. It feeds back into the very probabilities it's supposed to be measuring.
On Polymarket, reflexivity is everywhere. And it's where the deepest edges live.
The mechanism:
Reflexivity emerges when three conditions overlap on a market:
1. Visible price movement (the chart is being watched)
2. Thin liquidity (small volume moves price meaningfully)
3. Narrative attachment (price is interpreted as evidence)
When all three converge, a feedback loop forms. A price move generates attention. Attention generates trades. Trades move the price further. The original move - which may have been pure noise - gets retroactively justified by the very market response it triggered.

This breaks every standard probabilistic assumption - but it's also where the most asymmetric trades live. Because reflexive moves are mean-reverting once the loop breaks, and the timing of the break is partially predictable.
Why it works on Polymarket specifically:
Three structural features of Polymarket make reflexivity unusually strong:
On-chain visibility - every whale entry is public. When a $50K wallet buys, smaller wallets can see it instantly. The signal becomes the trade.
Twitter narrative coupling - Polymarket prices are quoted in real-time on X. Every meaningful move triggers commentary, which triggers more moves.
Thin tail liquidity - outside the top 20 markets, order books are shallow. A $5K position can move price 3-5¢, which is enough to start a reflexive cascade.
Together, these create a market where price moves are sometimes the only news - and the only edge is knowing when the loop is real and when it's about to break.
The execution framework:
Quants approach reflexivity in two opposite ways depending on the phase of the loop:
Phase 1 - Riding the loop (early)
When a reflexive cascade begins, the trade is to enter early and exit before the loop saturates. The signal is:
Whale entry visible on-chain (>$20K position into thin market)
Coincident X amplification (post crosses 5K impressions)
No meaningful underlying news to anchor the move
In this phase, you're not trading the event. You're trading the loop itself.
Phase 2 - Fading the break (late)
Every reflexive loop terminates. When the loop breaks, price reverts toward the pre-loop equilibrium with surprising speed. The signals that the loop is exhausting:
New entrants slowing (tx velocity peaking)
Spread starting to widen (LPs sensing risk)
Narrative becoming explicit ("everyone agrees this is going to 90¢")
When the consensus becomes the trade, the trade is over.
Why most traders never see it:
Reflexivity requires holding two contradictory ideas at once: the market is wrong and the market is moving anyway because it's wrong in a self-reinforcing way. Retail traders can't sit with this contradiction. They either capitulate to the move (buying the top) or refuse to engage (missing the entire cycle).
Quants don't resolve the contradiction. They trade it.
